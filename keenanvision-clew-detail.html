The Diagnosis
An FDE agent is only as good as the three layers of context underneath it.

The reason "give every FDE an agent" is hard — and the reason it's defensible once you get it right — is that useful deployment context lives on three distinct levels. Miss any one and you are back to a generic chatbot that stalls in the 95%.

Business context
The operations, processes and terminology of this customer. Where the exceptions hide. The layer generic models never learn on their own.
Architecture context
Your target systems, their metadata, and the accumulated best practice for building on them well. What "good" looks like on this stack, and which patterns blow up in production.
Build context
How to configure, test and ship the agent, and integrate it without breaking anything. Where plain-English intent becomes a deployed, governed thing.
The compounding effect
Ontologies built for one domain extend into the next. Each deployment leaves reusable building blocks. The advantage accrues across the system, not inside any single project.

Almost every ISV attempting this starts by dumping documentation into a vector store and wiring it to a model. That gets you plausible answers, not grounded ones — and no way to tell the difference at scale. What is actually required is a governed graph, an inheritance model that separates what you own from what your partners and customers own, skills that make the agent behave, a curation process that assumes nothing is trustworthy until a human says so, and an evaluation loop that proves it.

That is a two-year build. We have already done it.

Pillar 02 · In Detail
Three layers, one spine: from what a practitioner wants to what your platform does.

The core of Clew is a three-layer map. Everything else in the graph — governance, engagement, context intelligence — hangs off this spine rather than living as a separate tree.

Layer 1 — Business Intent
The practitioner's own language. "Unify customer profiles." BusinessIntent → USES_TERM → VocabularyTerm.
Layer 2 — Semantic Structure
The ontological middle. SemanticEntity defines canonical concepts; ContextPattern defines validated plays. Reached via EXPRESSES_AS.
Layer 3 — Platform Binding
What actually runs on your product. PlatformObject → INVOKES → EngineFunction. Reached via MAPS_TO and RECOMMENDS.
The bridge node
ContextPattern is the only node type spanning all three layers. That polymorphism is why it is the primary retrieval target — enter the graph from any layer, land on the pattern that bridges them.
The identity contract is what makes it an ISV product rather than a project.

Every inheritable node carries five fields: domainKey, stableKey, qualifiedKey, displayId and overrideMode. That last one is the commercial mechanism. Your canonical library is the base. Your partners supplement and refine it with vertical accelerators and their own decision records. Each customer engagement can replace or suppress at the project level for regulatory or local constraints — without ever forking your canon.

Pillar 05 · In Detail
Zero-trust curation: nothing is canon until a human says so.

"No content is better than incorrect content. An empty page is acceptable. A page filled by a Tier-3 guess is not."

Most context programmes fail on the way in, not the way out. They ingest everything, trust the average, and discover eighteen months later that the agent is confidently citing a deprecated pattern. Clew assumes the opposite: every claim is untrusted until it has earned a status, and the earning is mechanical.

Gate 1 — Ingestion validation
Decisions captured in conversation are scored for completeness and coherence against a threshold. Pass or fail, recorded.
Gate 2 — Promotion
Traces group by decision point into a candidate, tested on volume, outcome coverage and consensus before it can advance.
Gate 3 — Governance review
Alignment, contradiction and sign-off. Only on approval does a decision record become authoritative and enter production retrieval.
Quality flags at every gate
Issues attach to the gate where they were raised, so failure is diagnosable rather than mysterious.
The gate an agent cannot open

Every node in an engagement twin carries a provenance value — confirmed or simulated — and a review status that moves one way only: from draft or agent-authored to human-ratified or rejected. Both end states are terminal.

And ratification is structurally excluded from the agent's tool surface. It is not hidden behind a confirmation prompt or a permission flag — the verb does not exist in the catalogue the model can see, and the tool policy is deny-by-default with a completeness guard that fails the build if a new capability appears without a conscious allow-or-deny decision. Humans ratify through a console, under a named session. An agent physically cannot satisfy its own review gate.

"The one verb whose absence from the agent surface is a feature."

This is the difference between a trust layer and trustworthy content. A safety filter measures whether the agent did something bad. It says nothing about whether the agent did the business task correctly. Your FDE agent will be quoted in front of your customer's architecture review board. It needs the second thing.

Pillar 03 · In Detail
The eval loop is not quality control. It is the product.

For most enterprise software, evaluation is adjacent — you can imagine the product existing without it, just worse. Not here. An FDE agent is the calibrated mapping between practitioner intent and system response. Take away the calibration and you don't have a degraded version. You have nothing.

GA Gate — Metric 1
≥ 80%
Of corpus queries must return Tier-2-or-better: a confident single pattern, or ranked alternatives with explicit trade-off framing. Below that, it doesn't ship.
GA Gate — Metric 2
Blind win
The agent must beat base Claude in a blind comparison, adjudicated under a published protocol. If your context layer can't beat the raw model, you don't have a context layer.
Corpus discipline
Hand-built
Questions are authored by architects from real understanding — never scraped from chat logs or reverse-engineered from the patterns they're meant to test. A named Eval Manager approves every addition.
The compounding move
Gaps → roadmap
When a check fails because the cited entity isn't in the graph, that isn't an eval failure. It's a curation deliverable. Every cycle produces a prioritised list of what to build next.

This is the loop that makes a small expert pool viable. You cannot hire thirty thousand contractors to manufacture ground truth for your platform, and you shouldn't want to. One hour of your best architect's time, spent authoring a rubric rather than grading, displaces roughly a hundred hours downstream — and produces three compounding outputs: durable rubric IP, a better judge, and the next round of curation targets.
