Straight Answers

The questions ISV leaders ask in the first meeting.

Why not just point a model at our documentation?

You'll get plausible instead of grounded, with no way to tell them apart. A graph knows whether the entity exists, the relationship is real, and the claim has a source — embeddings only know what looks similar.

Why couldn't we build this ourselves?

You could. It took us two years, and the artifact that matters isn't code — it's 107 architecture decisions, most recording a wrong turn first. Meanwhile the people who'd build it are the experts you need deploying.

Does this lock us into you?

No. The graph, repositories, decision records and operating model are yours from day one, in portable form, with every choice written down. Phase 3 is explicitly handover.

What happens when the models change?

Nothing — that's the point. Ground truth in a versioned graph survives model, retrieval and rubric changes, so improvements compound instead of evaporating.

How do we know it's working?

We agree the gate up front: 80% of a hand-authored corpus strong-or-better, and a blind win against the raw model. If your context layer can't beat the base model, we'll tell you.

How do we stop it going rogue in a customer's org?

The tool surface is deny-by-default, and the build fails if a new capability appears unclassified. The verb that marks content approved doesn't exist for the agent — only a named human can.

Is this consulting with a technical coat of paint?

Fair challenge, so here's the falsifiable version: Phase 1 ends with running software answering questions about a real customer, with a score. If we can't deliver that, don't buy Phase 2.
